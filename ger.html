G.E.R. Statement

During my time in my undergraduate studies, I participated in multiple general education courses. Although they are not engineering focused, they contribute significantly to my ability to succeed as an engineer. This is due to two factors: Directly applicable concepts, increased knowledge base, and leadership development.

The increased knowledge component is the most direct to engineering success on a technical level. This is because the core talent I possess relates to finding analogies between distant fields, allowing me to not only develop novel designs but also iterate on them quickly by creating analogous workflows in the target field from the source field. This lets me work comfortably in environments that I have little direct experience in and rapidly acclimate to them. Without general education, I would have a much smaller base of knowledge to work off. From these, I have increased my knowledge in music [Introduction to Music Listening, whose concepts of filters and timbres have important consideration in signals], history [Introduction to Western Civilization II, from which previous decisions and tactics may inform the future], and psychology [Introduction and Motivation, from which I have improved my auditing capabilities and are directly applicable to AI]. This increased knowledge base also differentiates me from others in my field and was an important factor that allowed me to secure a high-profile National Lab research internship.

The other component is the leadership resources I gained from the psychology courses and my leadership class. Making an impression is hard, and with a world that is facing routine cyberattacks and ransomware threats, it's critical to be able to communicate effectively. This is especially true, as cyber is quite similar to business in that it is not entirely riskless, and justification must be properly given for actions that have potential for failure but are the correct course to take. Particularly, I found immense value in the leadership course on talking to people on pressure point topics as well as considering group dynamics to get the job done.

For these reasons, and more, I believe that my general education classes have been foundational to my growth as an engineer, as they round out my skillset and provide skills that allow me to bridge gaps, whether those gaps are between wires or people.
