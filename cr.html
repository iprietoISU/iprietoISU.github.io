A Reflective Journey: Navigating Your Cumulative Experience at Iowa State University

One of my favorite things in the world is learning new things.

I have rather vivid memories of being a child, growing up in Ames. It was a small town, much smaller than it is now. A lot of the time, there wasn't too much to do. But I had a few hobbies: Watching Star Trek (The Original) and reading a visual encyclopedia on Star Wars. Now, I wouldn't classify myself as being in the “sci-fi” sphere, but these pieces of media served a purpose. I wasn't primarily interested by the grand scale of things, or the complex storytelling that would occasionally be present (something that I appreciate much more in my adult years). No, it was a different kind of interest. Specifically, when I saw these shows, read these books, I thought to myself:

"This is possible. There is some way this is all possible." And I wanted to know how.

I started early. Building robots out of Legos, experimenting with the computer in the house. Around second grade, I was introduced to JavaScript. Sixth grade, and I was introduced to cybersecurity. I enjoyed these things immensely. In high school, I was already regularly on the ISU campus, working on control systems for full-sized competitive robots, and going to schools to help the next generation start out with their Lego Robots. A lot of my downtime was spent reading Wikipedia articles on anything I could find, surfing for hours at a time (A lot of people at that time were still under the impression of Wikipedia as a free-for-all, but I found it a valuable general reference that has proved it's worth many times over.) This boosted a particular way of thinking I have, where I can pull concepts from separate fields abstract them, and apply them to whatever I'm working on. I've refined and honed this approach throughout my entire education, and is

ISU is well known for its world-class cybersecurity graduate program, and when I discovered that an undergraduate major had opened a year before I was to exit high school, I knew exactly where I was heading. The next year, I was there. The first few years there were in the midst of the COVID-19 Pandemic. In fact, before attending, our school decided to cancel half of the twelfth grade. It would be a challenge to anyone, but it was a challenge I overcame. It wouldn't be the end of challenges faced at ISU, but it's exactly why I went. By facing these challenges, I grew each time. Managing multiple classes. Living away from home. Diving in headfirst into new fields.

One of the areas in which I grew the most is leadership. Up till about the end of high school, I had always considered myself as a great follower. Through experiences such as my software project class and my senior project, along with being the recipient of many scholarships, I have realized that I am not only a great team member, but a very capable leader as well. In the case of the software project, my team members were beginner programmers, with some of them working outside jobs. Most of the other teams would end up designing small android apps to solve useful problems. However, I brought together the team with an idea in a field I personally we collectively had absolutely no experience in: Game development. By the end, we were one of the most ambitious groups, having built an entire game engine off LibGDX and developed a small demo of our intended game to go with it. When presented with an impossible deliverable due to an assumption on how students would pick what project to do [which has been updated for future version of the course, from what I understand], we simply made it possible, From what I can tell, we created the only proper unit/system testing framework for LibGDX in general. Our project was only matched in complexity by a highly experienced team who were porting a game that they had already made. Without the experience and confidence I had gained through ISU, I doubt I would have been able to accomplish something like that.

Neither would I have expected to win an ISU Cyber Defense competition; an extracurricular I regularly participate in to practice real-world cybersecurity. But after I had decided to explore further the protection advanced firewalls had to offer, something that we had touched on in our cybersecurity coursework, I identified an opportunity. By figuring out an optimal configuration for the firewall and properly deploying it within the simulated network, along with some routing changes, our team remained uncompromised. And with the expert knowledge of the other team members, we were able to solve most challenges and secure the win. As a member of ISU's cybersecurity club, HACC, I made sure to provide the exact steps necessary in a club guide on how to deploy these systems at the ISU CDC so others can find the success we did.

These experiences paid off. I was able to enter a highly competitive government internship, and was introduced to a 40-year-old cognitive architecture, SOAR, used for the creation of AI that thinks and acts like a person. The idea was to figure out how this could be applied to cybersecurity and be integrated with modern LLMs. SOAR was not an AI as we know it today, based on neural networks and transforms. Instead, it's based on a complex fire-execute-revoke framework with a focus on hierarchical task decomposition. What ended up being necessary was a complete disconnection from linear thinking, as a single decision starts leading to multiple locations, each possibly modifying trees in their own timelines, and the mere existence of a decision point in time can affect decision points unrelated to it. This was all expressed in the form of ordered triplets composed of symbols with SOAR-specific meanings and where the presence of specific checks, something very common in regular programming, can change the entire meaning of setups.

It was, what I believe, to be one of the most extreme tests I have put my adaptability to. I fully immersed myself in it, and within a week, I had found success in using the architecture. Not too long after, I had a moment of insight in that although the symbols were extremely opaque to a person, they would be extremely easy for something like an LLM to understand, and one could theoretically be used to coordinate a massive threat-sharing network, with SOAR acting as an agentic intrusion detection/prevention system that could respond and adapt to a cyberattack happening on the other side of the world, applying the cyber knowledge I had gained from ISU. With this, I created a poster and presentation, as well as a paper with my mentor. This paper allowed me to become published before I turned 21, as first author.

ISU has prepared me extremely well for a future in engineering, through challenges of life and intellect, and to solve whatever I put my mind to. We live in a time where a lot of the things in those old Sci-Fi stories have become reality. Cell phones. Directed energy systems. Even a computer you can talk to like a person. The world is changing. Cybersecurity is a contionously evolving beast, one that will always be a worldwide issue and one that will always provide new and interesting situations. Now, I'm studying large-language models, and plan on developing my own at some point. Additionally, I have taken up electrical engineering (something I have always been interested in) and am educating myself with online resources about basic circuitry and have been working on building my own guitar effects unit. And now that I have been accepted into the ISU engineering master's program, I am excited to see where the future leads.


For the path I took to get here? I wouldn't change a thing.
